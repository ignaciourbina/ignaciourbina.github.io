Statement of Interest — Discussant, APSA 2026

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Political Science at Stony Brook University. My research examines how citizens form beliefs and attitudes about artificial intelligence and how these shape political behavior and policy preferences. I work at the intersection of political behavior, public opinion, political economy, and computational social science, combining experimental methods with computational modeling.

My active research spans three areas. First, the political economy of AI-driven automation: my job market paper introduces a conditional activation framework for when workers update policy demands in response to automation risk, using a pre-registered experiment and Bayesian finite mixture modeling. Second, AI and democratic institutions: I am developing an open-source agent-based simulation that uses LLM agents as synthetic citizens to test how deliberative institutional designs affect consensus formation. Third, public attitudes toward AI: ongoing work maps the multidimensional structure of AI attitudes across domains using nationally representative U.S. data. Additional projects address human-AI cooperation in strategic settings, misinformation susceptibility under cognitive overload, the effects of state violence on political trust in Latin America, and immigration labor market perceptions.

Methodologically, I work with survey experiments, causal inference, Bayesian statistics, agent-based modeling, and factor analysis. I have served as a discussant at APSA (2024), MPSA (2024), and the IPSA World Congress (2023).

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss panels on political behavior, public opinion, the politics of AI and automation, computational approaches to political science, democratic deliberation, or Latin American politics.
